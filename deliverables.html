Written Report Structure

Your report should be professional, clearly written, and suitable for presentation to financial decision-makers. Suggested length: 5-8 pages, single-spaced, 12pt font.

1. Executive Summary (½ page)

A concise overview of your analysis, key findings, and main recommendations.

Include:

Element Details
Problem Why interest rate risk matters for fixed-income investors
Approach Dothan model, Monte Carlo simulation (5,000 paths), portfolio analysis
Key Findings Effect of volatility, initial rates, portfolio risk metrics
Conclusion Risk management implications and recommendations
Length ½ page maximum

Example metrics to highlight: - Portfolio duration: ~[X] years - Volatility sensitivity: ~[Y]% price change per 1% rate change
- Stress test loss under +2% shock: ~[Z]%

Sample Opening: > “This project applies the Dothan stochastic interest rate model to analyze interest rate risk in a fixed-income portfolio. Using Monte Carlo simulation with 5,000 paths, we estimate zero-coupon bond prices for maturities of 1, 3, 5, and 10 years under baseline and stressed scenarios. Results show that a +2% interest rate shock would reduce the portfolio value by [X]%, with longer-duration bonds suffering disproportionately greater losses.”

2. Introduction (1-1.5 pages)

Content: - Definition of interest rate risk and its importance in finance - Overview of stochastic interest rate models - Why the Dothan model is relevant (and its limitations) - Project objectives

Key Points to Address:

  • Why interest rates matter (bond pricing, duration, convexity)

  • Limitations of deterministic models

  • The Dothan model’s simplicity and tractability

  • Real-world applications in risk management

Recommended Structure:

  1. Interest Rate Risk in Context (½ page)

    • Impact on bonds, banks, insurance companies
    • Inverse relationship: \(P \uparrow \text{ when } r \downarrow\)
    • Non-linearity (convexity): price changes accelerate at extreme rates

  2. Stochastic Modeling Paradigm (¼ page)

    • Why randomness matters: captures uncertainty about future rates
    • Contrast with deterministic approaches (constant-rate assumption limitation)

  3. The Dothan Model (¾ page)

    • Design: geometric Brownian motion \(dr_t = \mu r_t dt + \sigma r_t dW_t\)
    • Advantages: simplicity, lognormal rates, closed-form bond prices
    • Limitations: no mean reversion, potential explosive growth

  4. Project Scope (¼ page)

    • Objectives: simulation, pricing, risk analysis
    • Deliverables: report + code

3. Model Description (1-1.5 pages)

Mathematical Foundation (1 page):

The Model: \[dr_t = \mu r_t \, dt + \sigma r_t \, dW_t\]

Parameter Table:

Parameter Symbol Meaning Baseline Value
Short Rate \(r_t\) Instantaneous risk-free rate Variable
Drift \(\mu\) Expected percentage growth rate 0.02 (2% p.a.)
Volatility \(\sigma\) Standard deviation of percentage changes 0.15 (15% p.a.)
Brownian Motion \(dW_t\) Random shock term \(\sim N(0, dt)\)

Key Properties:

  • Always positive: \(r_t > 0\) for all \(t\) (if \(r_0 > 0\))

  • No mean reversion: rates can drift up or down indefinitely

  • Lognormal distribution: \(r_t \sim \text{LogNormal}(\log r_0 + (\mu - \sigma^2/2)t, \sqrt{\sigma^2 t})\)

Simulation Methodology:

Present the discretization: \[r_{t+\Delta t} = r_t + \mu r_t \Delta t + \sigma r_t \sqrt{\Delta t} Z_t\]

Explain: - Why Euler-Maruyama scheme - Choice of time step (e.g., \(\Delta t = 0.01\)) - Convergence properties and error analysis - Implementation details

Bond Pricing Theory:

\[P(t, T) = E_t \left[ \exp\left( -\int_t^T r_s \, ds \right) \right]\]

Explain: - Expected value across all paths - Discount factor computation - Monte Carlo estimation - Convergence and standard error

Include a figure showing sample interest rate paths.

4. Simulation Methodology (½-1 page)

Computational Approach: - Number of paths (e.g., 5,000 or 10,000) - Time horizon: 10 years - Time step: 0.01 years (approximately 2.5 trading days) - Random seed: 42 (for reproducibility)

Parameter Values:

Parameter Value Justification
\(r_0\) 3% Current market short rate
\(\mu\) 2% Historical average drift
\(\sigma\) 15% Historical volatility estimate
\(T\) 10 years Long-term horizon, covers full yield curve
\(\Delta t\) 0.01 years ~2.5 trading days; balance accuracy/speed

Validation: - State what validation was performed (e.g., lognormality test) - Report test statistics (e.g., K-S test p-value) - Conclusion: paths match theoretical distribution

Include figures: - Histogram of final rates with theoretical distribution - Comparison of simulated vs theoretical moments

Document the four tasks:

Present: - Table comparing prices at \(r_0 = 0.02, 0.03, 0.05\) - Key finding: Multiplicative effect (e.g., “100bp increase → -5% price change”) - Insight: Starting point determines entire distribution of future rates

Task 3: Portfolio Risk

Present: - Portfolio composition table (bonds, weights, current prices) - Portfolio metrics (duration, weighted maturity) - Value-at-Risk calculations - Example: “VaR(95%) = -$23,000” (portfolio loss in worst 5% of scenarios) - Example: “Expected Shortfall(95%) = -$35,000”

Include a figure: - Pie chart of portfolio composition - Distribution of portfolio values across simulations

Task 4: Stress Test (+2% Shock)

Present: - Comparison of prices before and after shock - Portfolio loss: e.g., “-$145,000 (-14.5%)” - Individual bond impacts (showing why long bonds hurt most) - Interpretation: Duration approximation vs actual loss

Include a figure: - Bar chart showing bond price changes - Table of losses by bond and aggregate

Summary of Risk Findings: - Portfolio duration: [X] years - Expected loss from 1% rate increase: [X]% - Maximum loss in 95th percentile: $[X] - Recommendations for risk mitigation

7. Conclusions (½-1 page)

Main Takeaways:

  1. Volatility Paradox: Higher interest rate uncertainty increases bond prices due to convexity
  2. Duration Relevance: Portfolio duration ([X] years) determines first-order rate sensitivity
  3. Nonlinearity: Convexity adjustments matter for large shocks beyond duration approximation
  4. Model Validation: Simulated paths match theoretical lognormal distribution

Limitations:

  • Dothan model lacks mean reversion (unrealistic for long horizons)
  • No credit risk (assumed risk-free bonds)
  • No liquidity constraints
  • Parameter uncertainty not quantified

Applications & Recommendations:

  1. For Banks: Use to set exposure limits on fixed-income portfolio
  2. For Insurance Companies: Assess liability matching and hedge requirements
  3. For Investors: Inform bond allocation and portfolio rebalancing decisions
  4. Extensions: Calibrate to real market data, compare other models (Vasicek, CIR)

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

❌ Not validating simulation: Always check distribution of final rates
❌ Ignoring numerical precision: Small dt → large arrays; balance accuracy/memory
❌ Forgetting random seed: Results should be reproducible
❌ Slow Python loops: Use NumPy vectorization
❌ Mis-specifying Bond Pricing: Discount factors computed incorrectly
❌ Vague interpretations: Explain why results matter, not just what happened
❌ Insufficient validation: Compare to theory, check edge cases

❌ **Ignoring numerical precision**: Small dt → large arrays; balance accuracy/memory  
❌ **Forgetting random seed**: Results should be reproducible  
❌ **Slow Python loops**: Use NumPy vectorization  
❌ **Mis-specifying Bond Pricing**: Discount factors computed incorrectly  
❌ **Vague interpretations**: Explain *why* results matter, not just *what* happened  
❌ **Insufficient validation**: Compare to theory, check edge cases
