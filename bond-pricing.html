Introduction

Bond pricing under stochastic interest rates is more complex than under constant rates, because the future interest rates that discount the bond’s cashflows are themselves uncertain. This section develops the valuation framework and demonstrates how to estimate bond prices using Monte Carlo simulation.

Zero-Coupon Bonds: Fundamental Concepts

A zero-coupon bond is a bond that pays no coupons and returns only a single payment (face value \(F\)) at maturity \(T\). It is the building block for bond valuation.

Notation

Symbol Definition Units
\(P(t, T)\) Bond price at time \(t\) for maturity \(T\) Currency ($)
\(F\) Face value (principal) Currency (typically $100 or $1000)
\(r_s\) Short-term interest rate at time \(s\) Annual rate (e.g., 0.03 = 3%)
\(T\) Maturity date Years
\(t\) Current time Years (usually \(t = 0\) for today)
\(E_t[\cdot]\) Expectation operator Conditional on time \(t\) information

Bond Pricing Under Deterministic Rates

If interest rates were constant at level \(r\), the bond price would be:

\[P(0, T) = \frac{F}{(1+r)^T}\]

This is straightforward discounting—the face value is discounted back at the constant rate.

Bond Pricing Under Stochastic Rates

When rates are stochastic (uncertain), the bond price is the expected present value of the terminal payoff, discounted at stochastic rates:

\[P(t, T) = E_t \left[ \exp\left( -\int_t^T r_s \, ds \right) \right]\]

Where: - \(E_t[\cdot]\) = Expectation conditional on information at time \(t\) - \(\int_t^T r_s \, ds\) = Cumulative interest rate from \(t\) to \(T\) - The exponential of the negative integral is the stochastic discount factor

Key Insight: The bond price is not just the discounted payoff at a single rate, but rather an average over all possible future interest rate paths, weighted by their probability.

Closed-Form Solutions for the Dothan Model

For the Dothan model, there exists a closed-form solution for zero-coupon bond prices. This is one of the model’s attractions.

The Bond Pricing Formula

Under the Dothan model with risk-neutral parameters \(\mu^Q\) and volatility \(\sigma\), the zero-coupon bond price is:

\[P(t, T) = \exp\left\{ -\frac{r_t}{\mu^Q} \left[ \exp(\mu^Q(T-t)) - 1 \right] \right\}\]

Alternatively, using the original drift \(\mu\):

\[P(t, T) = \left( \frac{r_T}{r_t} \right)^{-\lambda} \cdot \text{[adjustment for volatility]}\]

Where \(\lambda\) is related to the market price of interest rate risk.

Practical Observation

For numerical work, it’s often easier to use Monte Carlo simulation rather than memorizing closed forms, especially as models become more complex.

Monte Carlo Bond Pricing

Method 1: Simulation-Based Pricing

The bond price can be estimated by:

  1. Simulate many interest rate paths from time \(t\) to time \(T\)
  2. Accumulate the integral \(\int_t^T r_s \, ds\) along each path
  3. Compute the discount factor \(\exp(-\int_t^T r_s ds)\) for each path
  4. Average the discount factors across all paths

Algorithm

Input: Current time t, maturity T, current rate r_t, number of paths M
Output: Estimated bond price P(t,T)

Initialize: sum_payoffs = 0

For each path m = 1 to M:
    Simulate the interest rate path from t to T
    Integral_m = 0
    
    For each time step i from t+Δt to T:
        rate = r_i (from simulation)
        Integral_m += rate * Δt
    
    Discount_m = exp(-Integral_m)
    sum_payoffs += Discount_m

Bond_Price = sum_payoffs / M

Code Implementation

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

class DothanBondPricer:
    """Bond pricing under the Dothan model using Monte Carlo"""
    
    def __init__(self, r0, mu, sigma):
        """
        Parameters:
        -----------
        r0 : float
            Initial short rate
        mu : float
            Drift parameter
        sigma : float
            Volatility parameter
        """
        self.r0 = r0
        self.mu = mu
        self.sigma = sigma
    
    def price_bond(self, T, maturity, dt=0.01, n_paths=10000, seed=None):
        """
        Price a zero-coupon bond using Monte Carlo
        
        Parameters:
        -----------
        T : float
            Current time (typically 0)
        maturity : float
            Bond maturity (years from now)
        dt : float
            Time step for discretization
        n_paths : int
            Number of Monte Carlo paths
        seed : int, optional
            Random seed
        
        Returns:
        --------
        bond_price : float
            Estimated bond price
        bond_price_std : float
            Standard error of estimate
        """
        if seed is not None:
            np.random.seed(seed)
        
        n_steps = int(maturity / dt)
        discount_factors = np.zeros(n_paths)
        
        # Simulate paths
        for path in range(n_paths):
            r = self.r0
            cumulative_integral = 0
            
            for step in range(n_steps):
                Z = np.random.standard_normal()
                drift = self.mu * r * dt
                diffusion = self.sigma * r * np.sqrt(dt) * Z
                r = r + drift + diffusion
                r = max(r, 1e-6)  # Ensure positive rates
                cumulative_integral += r * dt
            
            discount_factors[path] = np.exp(-cumulative_integral)
        
        bond_price = np.mean(discount_factors)
        bond_price_std = np.std(discount_factors) / np.sqrt(n_paths)
        
        return bond_price, bond_price_std
    
    def price_bonds_across_maturities(self, maturities, dt=0.01, n_paths=10000):
        """
        Price bonds for multiple maturities
        
        Parameters:
        -----------
        maturities : array-like
            Bond maturities in years
        dt : float
            Time step
        n_paths : int
            Number of paths
        
        Returns:
        --------
        prices : dict
            Dictionary with maturity -> price pairs
        """
        prices = {}
        for T in maturities:
            price, std = self.price_bond(0, T, dt, n_paths, seed=42)
            prices[T] = price
        
        return prices

Method 2: Exact Analytical for Dothan Model

For the Dothan model, a semi-analytical formula exists. Using the discretized approximation:

For a short time period \(\Delta t\), the bond price at the next step is:

\[P(t+\Delta t, T) \approx \exp\left( -r_t \Delta t \right) \cdot E_t[P(t+\Delta t, T)]\]

This allows exact (to discretization error) pricing by working backward through the tree.

Coupon-Bearing Bond Pricing

Extension to Coupon Bonds

For a coupon-bearing bond paying coupons \(C\) at times \(t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\) and face value \(F\) at maturity:

\[P(0, T) = E \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1 + r_{t_i})^{T_i}} + \frac{F}{(1+r_T)^T} \right]\]

In Monte Carlo:

def price_coupon_bond(r0, mu, sigma, T, coupon_rate, face_value, dt, n_paths):
    """
    Price a coupon-bearing bond
    C = coupon_rate * face_value
    """
    discount_factors = np.zeros(n_paths)
    coupon = coupon_rate * face_value
    
    for path in range(n_paths):
        r = r0
        pv = 0
        
        # Discount all coupons and principal
        for t in np.arange(0, T, coupon_freq):
            r_next = r + mu * r * dt + sigma * r * np.sqrt(dt) * np.random.standard_normal()
            discount = np.exp(-r * coupon_freq)
            pv += coupon * discount
        
        # Add principal
        final_discount = np.exp(-r * T)
        pv += face_value * final_discount
        
        discount_factors[path] = pv
    
    return np.mean(discount_factors)

Duration and Convexity

Modified Duration

The modified duration measures bond price sensitivity to small rate changes:

\[D_{\text{mod}} = -\frac{1}{P} \frac{dP}{dr}\]

For a bond, this is approximately:

\[\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \cdot P \cdot \Delta r\]

Example: A bond with modified duration of 5 and price $100 will lose approximately $5 when rates rise by 1%.

Convexity

Convexity accounts for the non-linearity:

\[\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \cdot P \cdot \Delta r + \frac{1}{2} C \cdot P \cdot (\Delta r)^2\]

Long-duration bonds gain more when rates fall than they lose when rates rise equally.

Connecting Convexity to Stochastic Rates

Under the Dothan model with high volatility, bonds exhibit more convexity because there’s more uncertainty about future rates. This makes them more valuable (all else equal).

Factors Affecting Bond Prices

Factor Effect on Bond Price Intuition
↑ Interest rates ↓ Price falls Future cashflows are discounted more heavily
↑ Maturity ↑↓ Mixed Longer duration → more sensitive to rates
↑ Volatility (σ) ↑ Price rises Uncertainty increases value (convexity)
↑ Coupon ↑ Price rises More cashflow received earlier
↑ Credit risk ↓ Price falls Higher default probability

Practical Considerations

Convergence of Monte Carlo

  • High variability in small samples due to randomness
  • Standard error decreases as \(1/\sqrt{n_\text{paths}}\)
  • Rule of thumb: need 10,000–100,000 paths for 3–4 decimal place accuracy

Choice of Drift Parameter

  • For risk-neutral pricing: Use the risk-neutral drift (often 0 or calibrated from market prices)
  • For real-world forecasting: Use the actual drift estimated from data
  • This distinction is crucial: risk-neutral = pricing, real-world = forecasting

Discretization Error

  • Euler scheme: Error is \(O(\Delta t)\)
  • Smaller \(\Delta t\): More accurate but slower
  • Typical choice: \(\Delta t = 0.01\) (10 steps per year × 10 years = 100 simulations per path)

Summary

  • Bond pricing under stochastic rates is the expected present value across all possible rate paths
  • Monte Carlo provides a flexible computational method
  • Dothan model has both analytical solutions and works with simulation
  • Duration and convexity remain essential risk measures
  • Volatility increases bond prices due to convexity (optionality value)

$$\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \cdot P \cdot \Delta r + \frac{1}{2} C \cdot P \cdot (\Delta r)^2$$

Long-duration bonds gain more when rates fall than they lose when rates rise equally.

### Connecting Convexity to Stochastic Rates

Under the Dothan model with high volatility, bonds exhibit **more convexity** because there's more uncertainty about future rates. This makes them more valuable (all else equal).

## Factors Affecting Bond Prices

| Factor | Effect on Bond Price | Intuition |
|--------|-----|-----------|
| ↑ Interest rates | ↓ Price falls | Future cashflows are discounted more heavily |
| ↑ Maturity | ↑↓ Mixed | Longer duration → more sensitive to rates |
| ↑ Volatility (σ) | ↑ Price rises | Uncertainty increases value (convexity) |
| ↑ Coupon | ↑ Price rises | More cashflow received earlier |
| ↑ Credit risk | ↓ Price falls | Higher default probability |

## Practical Considerations

### Convergence of Monte Carlo

- **High variability** in small samples due to randomness
- **Standard error** decreases as $1/\sqrt{n_\text{paths}}$
- Rule of thumb: need 10,000–100,000 paths for 3–4 decimal place accuracy

### Choice of Drift Parameter

- For **risk-neutral pricing**: Use the risk-neutral drift (often 0 or calibrated from market prices)
- For **real-world forecasting**: Use the actual drift estimated from data
- This distinction is crucial: risk-neutral = pricing, real-world = forecasting

### Discretization Error

- **Euler scheme**: Error is $O(\Delta t)$
- **Smaller $\Delta t$**: More accurate but slower
- Typical choice: $\Delta t = 0.01$ (10 steps per year × 10 years = 100 simulations per path)

## Summary

- **Bond pricing** under stochastic rates is the expected present value across all possible rate paths
- **Monte Carlo** provides a flexible computational method
- **Dothan model** has both analytical solutions and works with simulation
- **Duration and convexity** remain essential risk measures
- **Volatility increases bond prices** due to convexity (optionality value)
